Misc

Six concepts of Computer Architecture



Refer to Prof.Mudge's homepage. There are only six distinct concepts used to design computers, apart from technology advances that reappear in many guises over and over again:
  1. Locality – spatial & temporal
    e.g. caches
  2. Prediction – no state change
    e.g. branch prediction
  3. Speculation – state change
    e.g. prefetching
  4. Indirection
    e.g. virtualization
  5. Parallelism
    e.g. pipelining, OoO, vectors
  6. Specialization
    e.g. GPUs, accelerators

The 12 Commandments of Synchronization



Refer to Prof.Gun's article. There are 12 interesting but important commandments one need to bear in mind when trying to deal with multi-threading and synchronization problem:
  1. Commandment 0:
    Thou shalt live and die by coding conventions for synchronization.
  2. Commandment 1:
    Thou shalt name your synchronization variables properly.
  3. Commandment 2:
    Thou shalt not violate the abstraction boundaries provided by synchronization primitives, nor shalt thou try to change the semantics of wellestablished synchronization primitives, and thou shall look with disdain upon he who does.
  4. Commandment 3:
    Thou shalt use monitors and condition variables instead of semaphores whenever possible.
  5. Commandment 4:
    Thou shalt not mix semaphores and condition variables.
  6. Commandment 5:
    Thou shalt not busy-wait.
  7. Commandment 6:
    All shared state must be protected.
  8. Commandment 7:
    Thou shalt grab the monitor lock upon entry to, and release it upon exit from, a procedure. (Never sleep with a held lock!)
  9. Commandment 8:
    Honor thy shared data with an invariant, which your code may assume holds when a lock is successfully acquired and your code must make true before the lock is released.
  10. Commandment 9:
    Thou shalt cover thy naked waits. (Remember your cv and its condition!)
  11. Commandment 10:
    Thou shalt guard your wait predicates in a while loop. Thou shalt never guard a wait statement with an if statement.(if statement can't deal with spurious wake and mutex priority.)
  12. Commandment 11:
    Thou shalt not split predicates.
  13. Commandment 12:
    Thou shalt help make the world a better place for the creator’s mighty synchronization vision.
